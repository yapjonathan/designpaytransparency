How to get started on a project (such as writing up a case study)
First I want to clarify that this topic is on how to write a case study AFTER you have already finished a project. If you haven't started on a project then this page may not be as useful... You might want to check out How do I find a volunteer UX role? if you are currently on the hunt for a case study subject.
The "Storming" process
This is a term I "stole" from Tuckman's stages of group development that I think also applies nicely when trying to develop ideas.
Using this analogy, the "Forming" step is just figuring out what your goals are. In our specific case it could be to produce a case study that you can share to walk through your product design process.
After you have established your goal, you enter this "storming" process which I visualize as a big brainstorming cloud.
At this step there should be no "bad" ideas. Personally, I like to start with a "brain dump". I write out all my ideas, only loosely trying to organize them. At this point you can just ignore the doubts if something seems irrelevant.. if you think of it, jot it down.
This is a very important step as it is a way to just get STARTED. I believe there is a very similar technique in other creative endeavours such as writing where if someone worries too much about "getting it right", it creates a lot of inertia.
At this point you are trying to contain all these fast-moving thoughts and connections in your head, a feat that even sounds exhausting. The act of releasing these ideas from your head creates more "space" for you to focus on other connections and insights that could be hiding away.
I recall a Spongebob episode where he spends hours thinking about the perfect essay and only ends up with a single word on paper.
This first step of just getting ideas on paper (figuratively) is great for boosting confidence that you are not missing anything just because it seemed "dumb" from the get go. Even is something doesn't seem like a good fit, it is a good practice still to consciously note it and make the deliberate decision to exclude it in a later step.
This is what I usually do: Open up a new google doc and plop out everything I worked on in bullet points. I collect a local folder of all the different artifacts that I created and append my thoughts and notes to them.. all without worrying about if I want to include it in my case study or not.
Note: I think a good general mindset for any activities that involves "ideation" is to focus on generation in the beginning. There is a certain "cadence" or "zone" that comes from bouncing ideas off of each other to inspire others. This is why it is common to see tons of workshop techniques to encourage idea generation BEFORE any work is done to decide if these are "good" ideas.
The "Norming" process
Next after you have everything laid out before you, you can start trying to weave them together. Find a logical order that tells a story. The key here is to make sure each step INFORMS the next and there is a clear line you draw from the beginning to the end. Personally, in practice this is just me copying and pasting bullet points and notes around.
As you do this you will find pieces that don't seem to fit and you can deliberate about moving them around more or omitting them all together. You want to make sure everything you include actually made a difference to you reaching your goal. It is also at this point where you might start to see some gaps if one piece jumps to another with no logical bridge.
I won't go into the details on how you should structure your case study. Taking a look at How can I improve my portfolio? could help if you are looking for specific tips. The key point here is that this is the step where you start polishing the coal lump of ideas you generated into a the shiny diamond of work you are striving to.
How about "Performing?
This is where the comparison is a bit weaker. For the purpose of trying to realize a creative idea, the last step is really in the iterative feedback loop. How closely that relates to the word "performing" is up for debate.
But once you have cut down all the unnecessary fat and plugged in all the holes you can find, it is a great time to get some second opinions. One great tip I have is to "sleep on it". Looking at your own work a few days later, you likely will spot a bunch of improvements. Run the work through some peers, or through a mentor on ADPList.
In conclusion, you just gotta go for it!
One of the hardest steps is to just get started. If you try to approach it from the "storming" process, you might find the perceived barrier to entry a bit lower. Understand that the first pass will likely be shoddy but once you have it down, you have a something tangible to rework and improve on.
After all, it is hard to ask for feedback of something that is stored all inside your head. Once it is out you can start getting the feedback that takes projects from good to great.
