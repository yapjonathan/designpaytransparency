How can I improve my portfolio?
One of the most common mistakes we see as mentors for entry/new-grad portfolios is not enough continuity between the steps outlined in a portfolio.
The purpose of doing user research in the beginning is to gather insights that inform the design process. In your case study, we want to see your new insights being reflected in outlining the problem, the choice of what techniques you want to use, and what decisions were made.
Later on, the purpose of doing usability research is to either confirm these choices work or if changes need to be made. You really want to tell a story of how the design evolved and how the research led you to the final deliverables at the very end of your case study.
Of course this is not the ONLY reason you might be passed on for interviews (especially for entry/junior roles). There are many other factors at play, but here we will focus on the concept of storytelling and continuity for a portfolio project.
"Checklist" case studies
I often see junior/entry-level portfolios where it feels like research is being done to tick off a box, but the insight is just kind of dumped and forgotten. Sometimes there is no explanation of how the final deliverables even link back to the original goals.
This "formulaic" approach to portfolio case studies is examined in detail in this article, highlighting the problems found in many "bootcamp-styled" case studies often found in junior portfolios.
It is not about just doing a customer journey map, making personas, sketches, lo-fi, high-fi. Those are all just steps that facilitate the process. You want to weave the narrative with the results of doing all those steps and explain how they affect the next step you take.
It is common when you start out..
You might look at the portfolio of someone in a similar position as yourself who was successful in landing a great job and think "mine looks so similar.. I also identify a problem, do personas, show some sketches, etc." but the difference will likely be in how well their study connects. And that is something that hiring managers notice.

If that is the case, we should dig deeper into what is the VALUE you can derive from this exercise and then decide if it will be useful for your specific project.

Focusing on the "first" thing, not the "important" things
While scrolling through a portfolio, I see that the designer has tried their best to conduct some user research. I see surveys, affinity mapping, journey flows, and personas. This is fantastic! I see they have identified some goals for the project based on their research. For example, after talking to 10 sets of parents, a designer has identified a user need: "Parents want a way to store and share photos of their young children"
Awesome! I can't wait to see how they will address this problem.

Why are we randomly jumping to a login page? Where was it determined that the users NEED to log in to this app? How does this relate to the research and goals?
Sure the exception is if the goal stated was "to streamline account creation and onboarding process". If that is the goal, then starting from the log-in screen makes sense.
A thing to understand is that as a UX designer, the wholistic view of a product is very important... That is for a project, not for a portfolio. A portfolio piece is just a "highlight real" where you want to showcase your process, critical thinking, and problem solving skills.
Therefore, for your portfolio, if you are going to state the goals that were informed by your research, you need to follow through by addressing these goals when you demonstrate your process.
For example, some questions I have when I look at this are:
1.) Where does this navigate to? How did the user arrive at this page? Is this the Home page?
2.) What is this timeline? What does the "+" button do here when the timeline is active? How does the concept of a timeline address the user's need to "store and share photos of their young children"?
3.) Does the action the "+" button trigger change when you change the selection navigation below? Why is the "+" located here?
4.) In the same vein of the last question, why is there another "+" button on the same page?
These are just some (of many) questions I have just from a quick glance.
For this specific example, I want to see brainstorming, sketches, lo-fi/high-fi wireframes of whatever interaction you are proposing to actually address the user need stated earlier on. Bonus points for using your research to inform these decisions. An example is the designer could discuss the concept of a timeline, and how it works well in similar applications where the goal is to facilitate photo sharing. They could talk about how allowing comments on photos creates engagement and a feeling of belonging that aligns to the user's needs. Then they can show that wireframe and explain why this design is worth pursuing and testing.
"Cast a WIDE net but dig DEEP"
When you skim through a portfolio case study, the steps you see should feel like they are in order and stay in alignment with the goals you specify. Generally I like to recommend a preview of the bigger picture, but then focusing on something specific.

This example is just one way you could explain your thought process at the wire-framing step. The fun thing about UX is that there is never a single "right" way to do things.. as everything depends on your unique project and the variables around it.
One important thing to note is that this level of detail is NOT required for every single piece of your designs, otherwise your case study would be 20 pages long. By digging deep into one specific piece of design (that is ideally aligned to your goal), you share your process and capability, and now it can be assumed you are capable of repeating it across the rest of the designs.
This is much more preferable to simply dropping 50 wireframes in with no decision rationale behind it. All that shows is you are capable of producing wireframes, but doesn't demonstrate you can work through the process that makes wireframes so valuable.
"Show me the iterations"
One of the core drivers of UX is the "iterative process". This almost ALWAYS involves testing your designs with your intended users. A UX designer who never talked to a potential user of their product/service is a sad UX designer.
Make sure somewhere in the project you talk about how you interacted with users to VALIDATE your design hypothesis. Bonus points for clear annotation to clearly draw the line between the feedback and the designs.
Because UX is a continuous process, there is a lot of value in showing more than one round of feedback, insight, decisions and changes if possible. Similar to the cases above, this is another area where it is nice to dig deep into the specifics of a few designs instead of just glossing over many.
___
"How the story ends"
In general you don't need to dedicate paragraphs to every single methodology you showcase in your project, but a simple note describing your unique situation and a few sentences of why you are going through the steps of a selected methodology helps tie a portfolio piece together nicely.
Once you get to the end of the portfolio, the reader is hungry for a proper conclusion. Reflect on the goals you stated in the beginning, were you successful in accomplishing it!?
Here it is often really handy to give a quick refresher on what your goal is, how your design has accomplished it (or failed to accomplish it) and a few points of reflection on what you did well and would change the next time round.
It is key to also include the "proof" of the goal being accomplished here, as just saying "people loved my app" isn't very compelling. For example, this could just be some usability testing results where everyone was successful in accomplishing the tasks that are linked with your goals. Give the readers a nice ending, and it will result in a strong impression of you as a designer.
