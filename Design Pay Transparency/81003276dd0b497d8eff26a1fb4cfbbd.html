No dumb questions... but there are times you should google it first
A skill that almost ALL employers look for in one way or another is: Resourcefulness
Resourcefulness is closely related to problem solving, which is at the very heart of the UX process. There are many ways to be resourceful, but one of the most straightforward troubleshooting methods is simply "look it up."
"That is obvious," you might say. This article probably isn't going to be very useful to you then. To the people who are good at troubleshooting, it becomes painfully obvious if a question could have been easily answered with a quick search. I will list out four common ones I see as an example:
1.) How do I learn UX?
Do some research on this. The common paths are Self-learning, Bootcamp and College degree/certificate. There are hundreds of resources online outlining the pros and cons of each option. Take some time to self-reflect and come back with a more targeted question.
•
For example: Don't ask someone who has never done a bootcamp "if it is worth it." If you are asking a hiring manager what they think of bootcamps, preface the question with "During my research, it feels like hiring managers don't look favourably towards bootcamp grads in the last 2 years. What is your personal opinion on this?”
•
This laser-focuses your question on the unique perspective of the person you are asking while also showing you spent some time digging first.
2.) What is the best design process?
I don't want to sound like a broken record but... "it depends."
Some questions you might want to ask yourself first:
•
Who are you working with? What are their needs?
•
Timelines
•
Priorities
•
How hands-on are they? How involved do they want to be?
•
What resources do you have?
•
How much access to users for testing?
•
What tools can you access? How much will it cost if you don't have it?
•
Do you have SMEs (Subject Matter Experts) to consult?
•
How much time/effort do you want to spend?
•
How fast is your team's delivery cycle? Can you risk pushing out designs and fixing them next release?
•
What are you designing?
•
Consumer vs. Enterprise? Mobile vs. Desktop app?
•
How complex is it? Will you need deep IA?
Some steps of the "Design Process" are designed to answer these very questions.
My long-winded point here is that simplifying all of this down to one question shows a blatant disregard for the field of UX and implies the asker didn't even try to do some research before asking.
3.) How do I change the fill colour with X tool on a shape?
You will likely find 5+ Youtube videos of a helpful individual walking through whatever tool-related troubleshooting you require in the form of a tutorial.
•
Advice I have heard from managers: If you are troubleshooting an issue, it is best to spend at least an hour trying to figure it out yourself before asking your seniors. Not only do you learn how to be more efficient with troubleshooting, but you also ensure you don't take up the valuable time of your peers with a constant stream of questions.
•
If you still can't figure it out after an hour (flexible on your unique scenario of course), ask away. Make sure to quickly summarize what solutions you tried first. There is a fine balance of "giving it a shot" and "needlessly floundering," which you will learn to tread as you gain more experience.
4.) Is pagination better than infinite scroll?
Or some variation of is "X pattern better than Y pattern?". I specified an example here to walk through this point.
It depends! Are you designing a fast-moving feed or a relatively static list of reference-able items? If you look up this specific question, there are tons of forums with people debating this topic.
•
There are many pros and cons for different methods of showing large lists of items. At the end of the day, the “best" solution will be the one where the pros outweigh the cons for your SPECIFIC scenario.
•
Asking the question without researching which option would be better based on the pros and cons you found implies you were too lazy to look it up and are seeking the "easy answer.”
•
Instead of asking this question, summarize what your goals are for this design, how you hypothesize the user will interact, and list the pros and cons for the desired option. Then ask for a second opinion. You will likely get questions on how you came up with this as well as some insight you can work with.
You gotta walk the walk
You can claim you’re "resourceful" and a "problem solver." But if you follow that up by asking a question you could’ve researched first, then your declarations will be about as strong as wet tissue paper.
My advice is to consider "how do I phrase this question in a manner that indicates I have already tried troubleshooting it first?" Show you value the time of the people you ask by coming prepared after doing a bit of digging and don’t expect them to provide all the answers.
If you learn how to "prove your resourcefulness" when asking questions, you might eventually gain the reputation as the "person with insightful questions." This drives conversation and discussion.
