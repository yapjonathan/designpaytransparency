Understanding contracts
One of the fun things about life is that you will have to sign a contract with someone or some business. Be mindful that every service you do with someone is a contract. Contracts can apply across a wide range of services such as

Becoming a member at a gym
Contracts by default are not designed to be easy and interesting. They are often long, full of legalese terms and not easy for the lay person to understand. They can also range from one page to hundred's of pages depending on the type of the contract. An article from Herb Weisbaum at NBC news said most credit card agreements are too hard for Americans to understand. Nearly half (46 percent) of the 1,000 credit card holders surveyed said they “never” or “hardly ever” read their credit card agreements. That said, here are some general recommendations on how to read contracts and ensure you don't sign away your soul (yes, contracts have asked for this according to Huffpost).
1.
Try to read the whole contract. While this can be really boring, you need to understand what you are getting into. Special care should be taken for these following contracts, (please note this is not an exhaustive list, but these are ones you should definitely read end to end).
1
•
Rental agreements
•
Getting a car loan
•
Getting a home loan
•
Buying a house
•
Any agreement you sign with a school or an organization which involves loans, repayment, or income share agreements
•
Leasing or renting a car
•
Insurance contracts (you might be surprised to find out so many things are excluded)
•
Work agreements
•
Immigration documents
For the following items, you can often get away by skimming through the contract or reading the high level summary
•
Credit card agreements (check for late payment fees)
•
Getting a mobile sim (again check for early disconnection penalties and fees)
•
Setting up an internet connection (even this, I would just glance through the rules for what the penalties are for early termination)
2. If you have questions, ASK THEM. This cannot be overstated too much. Contracts are meant to be long and confusing. Hence, people get lost in the repetitive words circulated around. However, if you find something is odd or something triggers a red flag, ask the question. Here are some examples of red flags which you can look out for (again not an exhaustive list):
•
Work agreements or NDA's which are really restrictive about whom you can work with after you leave the company or put on your resume. This is a good read - https://www.benbarrettlaw.com/5-red-flags-to-look-for-in-an-employment-contract/
•
Rental agreements - Here is a good exhaustive list (https://www.apartments.com/blog/lease-agreement-red-flags-you-should-watch-out-for
•
Insurance - This is a good list of things to think about https://pcs.marsh.com/us/insights/blogs/insurance-policy-renewal-red-flags.html
•
School loans or agreements, such as income share agreements, where you plan to defer your payments until you get a job - While these can often reduce the burden for paying for education or a program while job hunting, there are a number of issues where you might end up paying a lot more over a period of time. With regards to income share agreements, which are really prevalent with the rapid proliferation of UX bootcamps, here are some good things to read through before you get into one:
— What is an income share agreement
— The True Cost of Income Sharing Agreements (ISAs)
— A guide to Income Share Agreements (ISAs) - Do note this has been written by a company which does ISAs, or Income share agreements. There is a strong positive bias. However, it does give good talking points about how your contract should actually reflect the agreement and not be all encompassing. — Terms to Look for in Income Share Agreements - Read this, seriously
•
Immigration requirements - While there is nothing you can do about these, understand every nuance of this properly so you are not found on the wrong side of the law. For example, a simple mistake of claiming to be an US citizen when you are a green card holder can get you deported.
If you are not comfortable with any of the contract agreements or you cannot get a clear answer, walk away. Walking away is hard, but can save you a lot of stress later in life. However, make sure you understand the risks to avoid a sticky situation.
3. You can always negotiate a contract - While not all contracts are negotiable, you can always adjust some contracts to remove parts that are offensive to you. Here are some examples:
•
Negotiating terms of your loans (home, car, Income share agreements, anything)
•
Rental agreement - You can definitely negotiate down details such as the initial deposit amount, the time period over which the deposit has to be paid, the late fees, or even the rent depending on the market.
•
Lease details
•
Your internet or phone bill plans (internet is always negotiable)
•
Your work contract - this can range from allowing changes to your contract to displaying your work in your portfolio.
4. Understand the risk of breaking your contract - This cannot be understated. Depending on the terms and conditions, breaking a contract can result in a number of unpleasant problems, such as your bills being sent to collections, hits to credit scores, significant financial loss, bankruptcy, imprisonment, and even being deported or barred from a country. Know the risks well and ensure you take the time to understand your options. Remember: contracts are a fact of life and millions of people have to deal with them daily. Do not be intimidated by them. With a little due diligence, they can help you a lot in the long term.
