How do I find a mentor?
A common piece of advice I see for professionals of all levels is to "find a mentor." How does someone go about meeting and securing one of these fabled mentors? You don't just walk into the "mentor store" and snap up a purchase.
1.) Identify what you want.
I touched on The importance of self-reflection and some tips to get started in another post, but one of the most important things to consider when looking for a mentor is to understand just what you are looking for. Sure, you don't need to have all the answers right away, but you need to at least have something to start the conversation with. Here are some prompts to get you started:
•
What is your background? What are skills and strengths you feel are very applicable to design?
•
What makes you unique and well-suited this work?
•
What are industries you are interested in?
•
Finance, healthcare, gaming, A/R, voicetech, security... they are all very different
•
Think about your culture and upbringing, do you have any goals for the location, team and values of your future employment opportunities?
Of course, these are just a few of many self-reflection points that you should consider when looking for a mentor. I recommend sitting down and writing out the answers to some of these prompts to at least have a starting point before you begin your search. You don't need to HAVE an answer, but you should have already put some thoughts into it.
2.) Think about what you want from a mentor
One of the worst things you can do as a prospective mentee is to reach out someone who has nothing in common with your goals and ask them to be your mentor.
Let's say you have a background in psychology and are looking to get into UX research in for the gaming industry in Australia. Then you reach out to a designer in Korea with a background in graphic design working in healthcare UI. This will only result in potential frustration as the mentor struggles with hypothetical advice. There is little alignment between their experience and the advice you seek.
You definitely don't need a 1-to-1 match, but you want to approach this relationship with a certain level of purpose and a reason to connect. It could be because you are both 1st generation immigrants in a certain country, you studied similar programs in school, or even if you are interested in their specific job role or industry. Just make sure you have something that relates directly to the potential mentor's experience.
3.) Be proactive - they won't come to you
A common plan of action I see are junior designers throwing posts in design communities stating "I am looking for a mentor."
This is ineffective because:
A.) Without knowing anything about you, very few people would be willing to take the plunge and commit to a serious mentor-mentee relationship.
B.) It comes off as lazy as you are expecting someone else to make the first move.
A recurring mentor-mentee relationship is a huge commitment on both sides. This is not something that most people would want to casually wade into. Therefore, it is generally very ineffective to reach out to people and ask them to be your mentor.
The goal is to strive for is a more "organic" way to kickstart this relationship. Slowly build it up with trust and create an interaction that benefits both parties. To do this, you often need to cast a wide net.
4.) Casting the net
Here is where sites like https://www.adplist.org/ come in real handy.
Once you have done the self-reflection steps and have a general understanding of direction, look for potential mentors on a site like ADPList to schedule sessions with people who you feel align to your goals.
Keep in mind you don't need to get it right on the first try. As long as you can go into the call with a general direction and effectively communicate it to the mentor, they can help you narrow down the scope of your goals or point you in the right direction.
As you talk to more potential mentors, you will have a better sense of who you "jive" with and if certain backgrounds and experiences really excite you. For the ones you really enjoy talking to, try to ask them if it is cool to book another session 2-3 weeks later to follow up on the advice they gave you.
They will likely say yes and that is your foot in the door to this mentor-mentee relationship. At this point, I would not pop the "will you be my mentor" question yet. That might scare them away. Try to book recurring sessions with them to update them on your progress and check in on the feedback from the session prior. You want to demonstrate your commitment to improving by following their advice and feedback.
5.) Reeling it in (sealing the deal)
After a certain amount of recurring calls, you might start to see the seedlings of a relationship start to take root. You might start looking forward to the next call to share updates. At this point, the mentor is likely looking forward to hearing about your progress and all your small victories along the way.
Depending on how you feel about the way things are going, it could be time to drop the "I really appreciate having you as a mentor through my design journey" or something similar in a call. This could be reminiscent to the first "I love you" in a fledgeling relationship, but is a milestone moment that takes you from "one-off advice calls" to an actual mentor-mentee situation.
6.) Keep it respectful
It's pretty common-sense, but as you reach out and talk to people, make sure you stay respectful of their time. If you are cold-reaching out to a prospective mentor, try to keep the initial sessions shorter with simpler asks.
A.) If you cannot make it to a session give the other party as much heads up as possible.
Send them an email with a short explanation and apologize for cancelling. 99% of the time it will be met with respect and understanding.
Similarly, if you are running late a few minutes, send them a note and they might be able to plan to wait an extra few 3-5 minutes for you.
If some extenuating circumstance made you miss a session, then send an apology email ASAP. Explain your circumstance and apologize. In general it is extremely unprofessional to completely miss a planned meeting, but accepting the responsibility and apologizing for wasting the other party's time is a good first step.
B.) Try to provide a rough agenda or talking points when you request the meeting
This will help the mentor prepare for the call. To be considerate, you should also confirm the mentor is willing to provide the kind of session you are looking for. For example, if you are expecting a deep portfolio review, you should ask them first if they are willing to conduct a portfolio review, and if they agree, send them the portfolio link ahead of the call.
Some mentors do portfolio reviews, some provide career advice, some would go through a mock interview with you, and there are many other services a mentor can provide. Make sure you communicate what you are looking for and leave it up to the mentor to confirm and prepare themselves to help you to the best of their ability.
C.) Watch the clock
If your session is 30 minutes long, try to keep it within that time frame. Keep your requests and expectations within the limitations of the time you have. If you are lucky, your mentor might be okay with extending the session a bit, but always make sure you stay cognizant of the time.
7.) Remember to keep your expectations realistic.
Once you have secured yourself a mentor (or a few), you just need to keep a few things in mind.
A.) You need to take their advice seriously.
If they give you feedback, then for the next session, you should have something to show for that. Show the outcomes of following their feedback or give an explanation of why you didn't follow it. If you continuously ignore their feedback, then it will alienate them and reduce their sense of purpose. After all, why are they your mentor if you don't listen to anything they say?
B.) You need to communicate your goals so they can make them their goals for you.
A mentor's purpose is to help you accomplish your goals. Without clear goalposts, this becomes an impossible task. You need to make sure you are clearly communicating what you are working towards so they can best help you along the way. Make sure to let them know if the situation changes so they don't waste time on advice that might not be relevant.
C.) They can only do so much, you need to provide the heavy-lifting.
Most mentors are not monetarily compensated for their time, so they will realistically only be able to contribute so much of their time to helping you. This means they are not there to hold your hands through the process and spoon feed you everything. For the most part, you need to take the drivers seat and come prepared with questions and things to share. For example, you should not go into a conversation saying "how do I make a portfolio case study" because it is clear you have not even tried. It is purely hypothetical at this point and it is very difficult for the mentor to truly add anything of value without spending hours learning about the project you want to do a case study of.
You need to come ready with something to share and then they can give you feedback and advice on how to improve it. Maybe they could do some high-level brainstorming with you. But they will not simply "have the answers" because they can't be expected to take the time to fully understand your project to give relevant, detailed advice.
In conclusion
Finding a mentor is not an easy task. It is a hard, time-consuming, somewhat iterative, highly subjective relationship-building exercise. But by keeping some of these points I outlined in mind, it can be a tremendously rewarding and mutually beneficial experience that can really help you work towards your goals more effectively.
Author's note:
Just wanted to mention that you don't ALWAYS need to build up a mentor-mentee relationship. It is 100% totally valid to reach out to a bunch of designers to get one-off career and portfolio advice. The thoughts and opinions I shared here is just for the times where you want to build a longer-term relationship.
