This is probably one of the most common questions I get. Unfortunately, I believe there is no "silver bullet" answer.
That being said, here are 6 common factors that come into play when looking at potential candidates. Each role will have a custom mix-and-match of these factors, but in general, aiming for a unique combination of them will likely benefit you in the job hunt, (at least for the first level of screening).
1.) Education
While many positions don't require a degree, it is definitely a preferred requirement for most UX jobs.
Good: Having post-secondary education
Having any kind of degree can be helpful, even if it is not directly related. It means you are capable of jumping the social and intellectual hoops of post-secondary education. Employers generally see this as some indication you have a "base-level" competency in writing, learning, and conveying ideas.
Better: Having the "right" degree
While technically you can enter the UX industry from any background, entry level positions will generally favor people who have a formal education in something UX related. This could be HCI, Industrial design, systems design, etc..
There is no discounting the advantage in spending 4+ years concentrating your education into the field you want to work in, especially because you likely do not have years of relevant work experience to set you apart.
Best: Going to a "prestigious" school
Like in all industries, there are a few really well-known schools that have a great track record of job placements. Some big names that come to mind are MIT, Carnegie Mellon, Stanford, UC Berkeley, etc.. While there may be a degree of correlation vs causation as well as self-selection bias, the reputation of a school or program is often something that impacts the on-paper attractiveness of a potential candidate.
2.) Portfolio
Almost all design jobs require a portfolio of some sort. While I have heard of people having success with a presentation deck portfolio, it is common for an employer to request an online portfolio to be submitted with an application.
Good: Having a portfolio
Almost all jobs in UX require some kind of portfolio in the application process. Having an easily accessible online portfolio is key. At the very least, a portfolio should feature 1-2 (or more) projects, have a section about yourself, your contact info, and access to your resume.
Better: Having a portfolio with solid projects
Quality is definitely better than quantity in a portfolio. Having 1-2 solid projects is very important. A few important factors that come to mind when judging the "solidness" of a project could be:
•
How much of the UX process did you cover? Why those steps?
•
Do the steps flow from one to the next? Is research being used to inform decisions?
•
Does it feel like a "complete" story with an introduction, goal, content, and conclusion?
•
Did you work with others? Were there real-world constraints and problems you dealt with?
•
Was the product shipped? Are people using it?
There are of course, many many criteria that a portfolio can be judged by. Here is a rubric of some popular ones. Additionally you can check out this FAQ topic: How can I improve my portfolio?
Best: Having great content AND presenting it in a visually delightful way
Content is the most important, but what separates the best from the good is the way it is presented. The most engaging portfolios focus on nailing the content but also take an extra step to deliver this information in a visual, engaging way.
This could be different for every project, but thoughtful use of colours, visualizations, photos, and content size/structure can go a long way.
3.) Work Experience
Even for internships and entry level jobs, the market is so competitive. Often applicants need some kind of experience to make the cut.
Good: Personal projects
Having the initiative to work on personal projects is great. They are a great way to practice applying the theories you learn. Because they are your own projects, you can massage them to align to your career and personal development goals. For example, if you want to focus on UX for AR, then you can do a personal project designing an AR app in Unity. The key for these is to try and interact with your product's users throughout the process of the project so you can showcase different facets of the UX research steps.
Better: Team projects
1
Many volunteer projects fit in this category. One of the most important skills in UX is the ability to collaborate and work with others. Ideally, this means both other designers as well as people from a wide variety of professions. Most product teams include developers, business stakeholders, and a variety of managers. This means in addition to all the skills you can showcase in your personal projects, you get to add an extra layer of complexity that comes along with working in a team.
Best: Co-op/Internships/Paid Freelance
Stacking on top of the team projects, to get one of these opportunities means you have toughed out some kind of recruiting process and you are valued enough by someone else to get paid to do all this work. It is also likely that you have already benefited from on-the-job training which implies you may need less of it in a future opportunity. As an employer, it feels like a "safer" bet to interview candidates who already have a bit of work experience.
There are definitely some experiences out there that look better on paper, as they act as a "shortcut" for recruiters. Sure, interning at a FAANG or similar company is not a guarantee that someone is a great designer, but it means the individual made it through a heavily selective process and emerged victorious while the majority fail. Generally, the "brand reputation" of where you interned is impactful, but usually only at the resume screening stage.
4.) Personality
As an entry level designer, it is hard to stand out via skills alone as those can be developed on the job. Some things such as a big ego, narrow-mindedness, and being difficult to work with are harder to change. Regardless of how well you score on everything else, you will likely struggle finding success if employers think you are not a team player.
Good: Confidence
This doesn't necessarily mean you must be currently brimming with confidence in your own skills. It could mean you have the confidence to trust in your own skills to the point where you know you can "rise to the occasion." This is usually split between believing in your own skills/potential as well as trust in the collaborative UX process. After all, if you can't even believe in yourself, how can you ask an employer to take that leap of faith?
Better: Confidence + Humility
Confidence is a double edged sword and it is easily taken too far into the realm of arrogance. The best designers believe in themselves, but also relish continuous improvement. This means staying open to new ideas, listening carefully to feedback, being self-aware and working well with others. The best designers are the ones who can properly balance confidence and humility.
Best: Confidence + Humility + Passion
The concept of "passion" is pretty overrated. I believe not everyone NEEDS to be passionate about their work. You can live a fulfilling life even if your passion and work never cross paths.
However, the "best" designers do tend to be quite passionate about their work. Many designers out there just have an innate drive to fight for the users so they can have the best experiences possible. Having passion, confidence, and humility is a triple threat and often these personality traits really shine later on in one's career.
5.) The X factor (what makes YOU different?)
1
I like this article where the author mentions "create a niche where you will be the top 1% through a unique positioning." What makes you different from every other applicant with a similar background and skill?
Good: Diverse personal experience
One of the biggest problems in many popular industries is lack of diversity. There are many factors of diversity (I touch on some different dimensions of diversity here). Some examples could be socio-economic background, culture, ethnicity, sexual orientation, etc..
Of course, that factor alone doesn't mean much. What is valuable is your PERSONAL story and how that impacts your problem solving, design thinking, and working style. Some examples I have heard include: volunteering at an retirement home, spending time in a competitive sport, or living in different countries to experience those cultures and learn new languages.
Better: Strong personal goals based off personal experience
It is one thing to have those diverse personal experiences, but what takes it to the next level is having those experiences act as the foundation for strong personal convictions and goals. For example, a designer who has spent many years working with the elderly can be extra-aware of many accessibility challenges faced by many older adults. This awareness could lead to more drive for action to address these issues that could easily be missed by people who might not share those same experiences.
Best: Track record of leading change influenced by your unique experiences
Walking the walk after the talk is what really "wows" people. It is very impressive to hear stories where someone took their own experiences and used it as the inspiration and motivation to drive real impactful change.
Building off the accessibility for elderly example: How impressive would it be if a candidate took their knowledge and designed a product that helps the people in the retirement home they volunteer at? It could be as simple as a larger backlit sign to showcase the daily menu in the dining hall after they noticed people were struggling with an older, poorly-lit chalkboard menu.
6.) Hustle
This last factor I have here is a bit of an outlier and I can't fit it into the good/better/best format. I think the concept of "hustle" has always existed, but only recently has it been thrown into the spotlight as magical solution of how to be successful.
Quite honestly, I don't believe hustling is for everyone. I believe it is completely possible to be very successful just by focusing on doing good work and letting your reputation speak for itself. However, in this age of social media, there are many undisputed advantages that come with being loud, present, and making sure your journey and success is plastered everywhere for everyone to see.
To hustle, one needs to be a bit vulnerable, have no fear of failure, and cast their nets wide. Many popular tactics include:
•
Cold messaging recruiters, hiring managers, and employees at places you wish to work for in order to have a coffee chat
•
Finding mentors and chatting with them as much as possible to glean advice and feedback
•
Coming up with your own content via blogs, articles, resources, or communities to become a thought leader
•
Volunteering in events and activities, getting speaker opportunities, etc..
•
Participating in many events, such as hackathons or design contests
Most of these tactics fall under the umbrella of "networking" (See this article on some tips on how to network when you start out). Hustling isn't for everyone because it is time consuming, can be quite exhausting, and not guaranteed to work. If done carefully and thoughtfully, it can open up a wealth of opportunities. Regardless of your relationship with "the hustle," I would recommend trying to keep in contact and on friendly terms with everyone you work with. The industry is small and you never know when you can help someone out or vice-versa.
1
The key to successful networking is finding relationships where it is mutually beneficial and both parties feel they are benefiting from the interaction. By furthering symbiotic relationships, trust is slowly built up and that can pave the way for many opportunities in the future.
Conclusion
So the unsatisfying conclusion to this question, like many other UX questions, is... it depends.
`
I listed many factors long with my opinion on good/better/best. If you have the privilege to score the "best" on every factor AND have tons of hustle, success is likely only a matter of time. If you only have a few, then try to brush up on the others.
At the end of the day, there is a lot of luck and timing when it comes to internships and entry level positions. But you can definitely up your chances by trying your best to stand out as a great applicant.
