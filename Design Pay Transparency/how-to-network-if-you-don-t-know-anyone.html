How to network if you don't know anyone?
2 backlinks
As you will hear from anyone with an opinion, the best way to get jobs is through networking.
The #1 best way to get a job is to be vouched for by someone you have personally worked with.
This is why you see so many senior designers repeating the importance of networking. For example:
You work with a team of 4 designers. 3 years later, half of them have moved on to work at companies you would love join. If you had a great relationship with those former co-workers and they truly valued your skills, they will jump at the opportunity to personally referr you the next time a role that you qualify for opens up.
How referrals work
Most big companies have a semi-formal referral process which actually asks for the relationship between the two parties. Ranked at the very top is "I have personally worked with this person in this job function." Not all referrals are equal. The "I met this person on LinkedIn" referral might be better than nothing, but pales in comparison with the personal connection.
Unfortunately, this strategy suffers from the chicken-egg situation of "what you just haven't personally worked with anyone else who has a job?"
Anyways, the question is about networking, not referrals?
True, you caught me.
It might be a bit bold to assume a big reason people starting out in their careers want to expand their network is to eventually get referrals to land jobs.
Aside from finding employment opportunities, there are tons of other benefits that come from networking that I won't dive too deeply into. The key to networking is to present a scenario where hopefully the person at the other end has something to gain from it as well, even if it is just feeling good about themselves.
This is one of the reasons why typically it becomes easier to network as you gain more seniority, as people will start eying your experience and connections as "value." But where does that leave you, someone with no connections and no experience?
Here are some strategies I suggest:
1.) Book some free mentorship sessions
There is a great resource called "Amazing Design People List" - https://www.adplist.org/mentors that has assembled a huge pool of designers ranging from juniors to management-level from a variety of industries around the world.
Even better, they are all offering to provide mentorship for FREE!
My biggest tip here is to be prepared for the session you book. While there are no "dumb" questions, there are some questions that you could easily type into google. I recommend you try to think of questions that either align to your personal situation or requires the insight of your mentor's unique background.
For example, if your mentor's profile it states they only work in EdTech, you wouldn't want to just ask them about FinTech because it is a completely different industry and makes them question if you even took the time to look over their profile.
You can also ask them to critique your portfolio. They probably have some suggestions for improvement and if they think it is great, they might even keep you in mind if a cool role pops up they think you could be qualified for.
Please DO NOT outright ask your mentor for a referral at the end of your first session with them. If you try to see it from their point of view, they just met someone and spent 30 minutes chatting with them. This is not enough even enough time to decide if a movie is going to be good or not, much less decide if they want to go out on a limb and vouch for someone. It puts them in an uncomfortable spot of wanting to be helpful but also feeling a bit "used" when it seems like they were only reached out for a referral and not for mentorship.
Make sure you follow up with the mentor after your session, thank them for their time, and keep them updated on your adventure! Many designers get into mentoring as a way to give back to the community and there is nothing that makes them happier than hearing they were able to help and follow along when their mentees succeed!
2.) Volunteer at an event
Unlike my other question about:
How do I find a volunteer UX role?
This is volunteering for non-ux work. This could be at a local hackathon, networking event, speaker series, etc. (Specifically you can scour Facebook, meetup.com and Eventbrite for these type of events). The design community is vast, welcoming, and is always hosting events. These events are mostly driven by volunteers who come from a variety of backgrounds.
Sure, you might be doing a bunch of random tasks like giving people nametags, moderating a speaker panel, or general event planning and management. But the people you meet will get a taste of what it is like working with you. Be courteous, professional, likable and most of all, reliable. These are soft skills that transcend any specific job that people will notice when working with you.
If you are lucky, you just might find yourself making small talk with a hiring manager at a company, or helping out someone who works in a role you deeply admire. Don't be afraid to tell people that you are open to new opportunities and you never know when something might line up.
The key thing to remember is to not go into a volunteer opportunity with the expectation that you deserve something. This is why it generally helps to volunteer for causes that you care about and can have a little bit of fun with.
3.) Join a design community
One community I will throw a plug in for is Design Buddies on Discord. I personally find the community there very kind and welcoming. The founder of the community is very active and invested in keeping it a safe, inclusive environment for asking questions and sharing experiences.
Online communities are a very low-pressure environment to ask questions, get second opinions, and general advice. If you encounter someone who has experience you are interested in, it is easy to reach out and schedule a coffee chat. This is much more effective than a cold-message through LinkedIn.
There are so many people starting out in UX that it is likely as long as you engage in the community, they will also engage back. You might end up making some friends in a similar boat that you can grow with!
There are plenty of design communities out there, all with a slightly different spin. Take a look and join a few. There is nothing to lose and you can always leave if you don't feel like you get anything out of it.
Networking can be as simple as starting a conversation
There is no need to be intimidated just because there is a specific term for this type of relationship-building activity. It operates on a similar basis as regular day-to-day human interactions. These three strategies above are just some ideas to get started, but there are many other effective strategies out there that you will get a feel for as you communicate and interact with more professionals.
The key to successful communication is trying to understand what motivates others, clearly articulating your points, and managing your own expectations. Sure, you can try cold-messaging everyone you see on LinkedIn, but you will be relying on lucking out on someone whose motivation perfectly lines up with yours.
Talking to people, getting to know their story, and sharing your own will likely forge stronger relationships that keep people invested in your success. Once you are in a position where you can help others, you will have motivations to do so, keeping this great wheel turning.
