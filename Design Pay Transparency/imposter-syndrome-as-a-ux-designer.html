Imposter Syndrome as a UX Designer

There is so much writing out there on this topic, so I will focus on some key points that help me wade through the sludgey feeling of thinking I am not good enough.
There is no official "career path"
Imposter syndrome definitely exists in all industries. It is incredibly common in UX purely because there are so many different winding paths that all lead to the same jobs. This is much less common for certain careers such as doctors, engineers, accountants, etc. Basically, any profession that has a certification board which requires people to jump through a series of hoops gives people more confidence that they have the baseline knowledge required to perform in a specific role.
Because design is a nebulous glob of "it depends" scenarios, it is very difficult to EVER feel like you KNOW what you are doing. It definitely does not help there are so many non-accredited institutions out there claiming they offer a complete UX design education... yet they all teach a different curriculum.
At the end of the day, the only foundational truths that act as my lighthouse are:
1.) There is no UX without the U (users)
One goal you should always strive for is to get your ideas validated by the people who will be using your product. There is no single "correct" technique for this. Even if you CAN'T access your users, you could fight for it and settle with the best functional equivalent in the meantime. After all, if you are just coming up with ideas and implementing them without ever getting a second opinion, what you are doing isn't really UX.
2.) Trust in the process
At the core of UX is the iterative process. There is no magical "skill" that allows a senior UX designer to just fart out a better idea than everyone else off the top of their head. They just have more experience that helps them balance tradeoffs more effectively. Even if you have no idea if your designs are any good, you can inch your way closer as long as you have clear goals and users to test with.
Literally EVERYTHING else they teach you are just additional tools on your belt involving how to work with different people, how to handle conflicting needs and wants, and different exercises to obtain answers that can help you ideate, prototype, validate and document. For example, just because someone is faster at a prototyping tool doesn't necessarily make them a "better" designer.
Therefore, as long as you are striving to include the first two points in your work, you are a good UX designer, have unlimited potential, and can continue to work on your supporting skills.
Dunning–Kruger Effect and the dangers of conceit
The Dunning-Kruger Effect is a prominent theory in psychology that seeks to explain the cognitive bias of illusory superiority. The TL:DR is the more inexperienced someone is, the more confident they tend to be.
The very fact that you are here reading a FAQ on impostor syndrome means you are probably sitting on the opposite end of the confidence spectrum right now. My argument is that is the preferable place to be, as long as you stay hungry for improvement.
"Staying humble" is generally a good M.O. Most people will function best when they believe in themselves and their skills, but not to the point that it makes them look down upon anyone else because they might look at things from a different perspective. The one trait that truly looks bad on a UX designer is conceit. No amounts of creativity or skills makes up for lack of openness to new ideas in this profession. Regardless of how innovative your ideas are, if the users don't agree, then you will slowly lose them.
Here is an example: Remember Blackberry? A tale of designers thinking they "knew what was better for the users," quoting no one would rather use a touchscreen over superior battery life and belittling the iPhone for its heavy data usage.
This "overconfidence" can be contrasted with your doubt that your work is any good. It's fine. Share your ideas with your users and their opinions can help improve the designs! If you remember the good ol' days you were confident and wonder "where did that go?", just think back to the D-K effect. It means you are now leveling up and finally understand enough to realize how much there is you still don't know. The downward slope right after you realize this is often when the "impostor syndrome" hits hardest, but understand after you flatten out, you can start working your way back up.
You are where you are for a reason
I won't focus too much on this point as many other articles out there do a great job at explaining this, but just wanted to end off with YOU DESERVE IT.
Many things in life required some luck, but you were prepared for that lucky moment. You worked hard to get yourself to a point where you could catch a lucky break. Of course, you should take some time to be grateful for your privileges too.. (I mention in this other article the tri-fecta of hard work, luck and privilege)
Even the most successful people you know probably had to deal with the same feelings of insecurity as you (I know I definitely do on a daily basis).
•
Think about how awesome it is you discovered UX as a potential career path.
•
Think about how amazing it is you have people you look up to and learn from.
•
Think about all the achievements ahead of you that can slowly build up your confidence.
You got this!
There will always be "haters" and petty people who tear others down to make themselves feel better. Maybe they are just too scared to jump off the "peak of false confidence." I have found it is best to focus on your own skills. Compare where you are today with where you were 5 years ago and leave them in the dust ;)
Anecdotes from a few senior designers in the industry on the topic of impostor syndrome:
"Everyone has imposter syndrome"
I think that it's important to recognize that everyone has imposter syndrome. Even people who present as being super confident don't always know everything (stack overflow is a thing for a reason).
There are good scaffolding/frameworks for learning, growing, asking questions and getting feedback in your career. Confidence is a byproduct of experience or ignorance
"It never really goes away"
Imposter syndrome, it never really goes away. I'm constantly afraid that people will "find out" I'm not a good designer and all the things I lack. But, something to think about is we're all humans and no one is perfect. That's why when I have coffee chats with people I encourage them to take my advice with a grain of salt. What I say isn't, the ultimate truth lol and to reflect if what I say is actually relevant to you. Plus, having worked with peers who are considered some of the best, they're not like incredibly different than the average designer. We're all learning and growing :)
"Acceptance and letting go"
A lot of imposter syndrome is letting your doubts cloud your reality, so doing honest and objective reflection is helpful even if it's uncomfortable to compliment your own achievements.
Try to understand what you're good at and accept what needs improvement. No one will ever be 100% where they want to be, because as we progress we strive for more or better. There's a lot of "letting go" that you'll need to do as well... if anxiety in the mix.
You might want to be perfect, be #1, etc... but you have to let go of striving for "being the best" when that's unrealistic. Appreciate and love the craft you're in by accepting that your career and growth is a personal process and not a direct competition.
You'll grow and prosper as you continue to improve, make mistakes, learn from them, and make good honest attempts forward.
