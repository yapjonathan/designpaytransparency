What are some common reasons designers leave their job in UX?
1 backlink
Here are some common reasons people in UX leave their current role for new opportunities:
1.) Lack of support in an organization
This usually boils down to not having access to users to test with. For whatever bureaucratic reason, all the requirements come from business or engineers. The designer has no support to contact users to validate assumptions with testing exercises. This leads to a rather "defeatist" mindset of "we tried our best with what we had," but that often feels empty because understanding users is a core tenet of UX.
A designer who has never interacted with their user is a sad designer.
2.) Other people not understanding the value of UX
They all think you are just a graphic designer and see you as a "mockup machine." Maybe you are not being involved in discussions about requirements because "this isn't a visual matter." They don't understand your timelines or your requests for more information because they see some of the work in the UX process as "irrelevant to design."
Often, projects just get tossed over with a week deadline which leaves no room for any insightful research. If you are just churning out artifacts and deliverables in a vacuum you might start questioning what are you really there for? It is important for designers to feel like their work is meaningful and serves a purpose, not just to check a box.
3.) Bad co-workers/managers
In any career there are divas. If you have the bad luck of working with someone with a huge ego, then that just sucks. Pretty similar to other professions here... whether it is belittling your ideas, micromanaging, purposely excluding your from things, spreading personal drama, etc., it's pretty hard to deal with this in the workplace.
1
Most of the time, it is not worth trying to "fight" bad co-workers and most opt to leave for a different opportunity.
4.) Lack of growth opportunities
This is also common in many careers. Like I mentioned in point 2, many people have no idea what UX people do. It is SO COMMON to see very junior people hired on as the "sole" UX designer on a small team just because "someone needs to make the mockups."
Of course, they usually end up floundering because they have no idea what they should do and don't have any guidance.
Additionally, senior designers sometimes find themselves in a rut where they feel they lost the "spark" of discovery. Perhaps all their work has just settled down to "production" tasks, such as generating design artifacts. Maybe they haven't talked to a single user in months due to some workplace politics. If they have absolutely no room to explore and potentially fail, they will feel they can no longer learn new things.
As people feel they have nothing to work towards or no mentors to help them improve their skills, they look outward to new opportunities.
5.) Lack of "clout"
This is also prevalent in many industries. Similar to software engineering, in design, big FAANG companies are put on a golden pedestal. You could be doing amazing work that changes the lives of many people, but because no one has heard of the company, you might feel others don't take you as seriously.
Because design is such a "thought leader" industry, the companies which invest the most into putting out public content are glorified and always used as a benchmark for how "good" a design is. In reality, good UX is very SITUATIONAL and what works for one product could be ineffective for another.
6.) Work-life balance
In my personal opinion, this is less of a problem in UX than in MANY other industries. The exception could be if you work in a consulting capacity or with a very lean startup.
Generally, a lot of UX work happens before products start being built. They are not subject to the same release pressures as other professions, such as developers or production artists/designers.
That being said, sometimes you get unlucky and stuck in a job where they expect you to live and breathe work. That never ends well. Most will end up burnt out and eyeing new opportunities.
7.) Murky Ethics
If you have seen that popular Netflix documentary The Social Dilemma, you might be aware a lot of UX research goes into influencing our daily actions. There are many designers who have left a role because as they learned more and more about how much impact they had on the LIVES of people, they grew concerned over the morality of their work. The impact of how effective UX was in polarizing groups over very divisive topics such as politics sets new precedents that many other industries never have to worry about.
Additionally, if a certain industry just doesn't appeal to you (such as oil and gas) because you care very strongly for the environment, then a job with that employer might not interest you regardless of how well it checks other boxes.
8.) $$$ and "Coolness" of project
I won't elaborate on this one. Sometimes designers leave for other opportunities because they just get a better package. Better benefits, more vacation days, stock options or cold hard cash.
People also have different interests. Some people just really want to work in gaming, social good, or machine-learning projects. The list goes on. This varies from person to person, but it is still important enough to make the list.
Let's end on a happy note
Often, the reason someone leaves a specific role is because they have found something else that aligns more with what they value! This could be purpose, pay, work-life balance, or team dynamics... basically the opposite of anything I listed above.
Everyone has a different tolerances and needs. What they prioritize (clout, money, ethics, etc..) might be different from a fellow designer. What is most important is to find what works for you right now. And who knows, that is subject to change with time.
