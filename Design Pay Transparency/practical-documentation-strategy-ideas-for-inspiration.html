Practical documentation strategy ideas - for inspiration
In many other posts I have highlighted the importance of documentation. However some feedback I have received is that the advice is a bit too high-level and it would be helpful to have some more practical tips/examples.
The reason there are not too many "practical" documentation tips out there is because this is a huge "IT DEPENDS" topic that can be quite unique for every single project. Therefore it is hard to boldly declare what good documentation strategy would look like because what works for one project could be completely inadequate for another.
Therefore I will approach this topic from more of a case-study point of view by outlining some practical strategies that have worked for me in the past, but you as a reader should take with a grain of salt. The following are some "principals" I consider when deciding how I want to approach documentation with a UX project.
The "hit by a bus" rule
A popular concept in science is the idea of repeatability which states that if an experiment is repeated with the same variables, the results should be the same. That is also a belief in UX, but to a lesser degree as rarely are these "variables" exactly the same as preferences change over time.
However, if you are doing your job, and one day you suddenly are unable to continue, could your colleagues take what you have and continue the project? Would they be able to figure out what your workflow was, what informed the decisions you made and be able to continue down your path?
While some of the things that factor into this hypothetical is out of your control, good documentation could really help smooth out any potential transition of work that you work on.
In a less morbid vein, perhaps you could be getting 2 new co-workers and you need to get them up to speed on everything you worked on. Would you need to spend weeks walking them through every single piece of your work or is there a resource that they can consult on their own time to gain a baseline understanding of the project and come to you with questions after?
The concept of "cover your own butt"
Memories fade. Small changes and obvious decisions today eventually add up and always result in some sort of consequence, good or bad.
One of the main purposes of documentation is to have a trail of why something ended up the way it did. If something was successful, it is nice to understand the steps and decisions as well as the rationale and actions that lead to the success. Being able to reflect on success allows people to identify patterns that can inform future success.
If something didn't quite work out (the more common route), then it is equally important to trace through the series of events and start to identify why things failed to avoid making the same mistakes in the future.
A COMMON scenario I hear from junior designers is:
"I feel that I am always being questioned on my design decisions and have to "prove" myself to a stakeholder. They offer different alternatives that is based on their personal point of view that is not backed by any research. In the end the products end up looking quite different when it gets implemented because they feel that they know the user better".
And the first thing I have to say is... this sucks. But I also follow this up with the fact that this is quite common in the industry. In fact, one of the primary job responsibilities of design managers and leaders is to "champion" UX so it is taken more seriously in an organization.
But you are not a design manager, what can YOU personally do in this situation to avoid getting stepped on by anyone who fancies themselves a better designer?
The answer is... documentation!
....
Let's run through a hypothetical example here.
Jane is working on a web-app for social fitness. An existing feature allows users to search and browse different user profiles to add them as friends. Some recent user feedback indicates that users are struggling to identify the right people to add and getting confused on how to add new friends.
Let's start our documentation by setting the stage for the project. Usually this will be tracked in some type of project management tool such as JIRA, DevOps, Asana, Trello, etc.. and be assigned a unique ticket ID (such as "FIT-APP-203").

We want to list out the project goals. This is how you will measure the success of this project, if you can successfully accomplish these goals. Not all goals need to be quantifiably measurable, but you should be able to produce a rationale at the end of the project on whether you think you were successful or not. Sometimes these are tracked by "User Stories" and "Acceptance Criteria".
Project Goal:
To design and implement a new UI that will:
1.) Allow easier identification of unique users when browsing
2.) Making the action to "add friend" more obvious and clear
Next, what I like to do is to create a sub-page for the project. This is where I will track the iterations of a design as we brainstorm, come up with ideas, test them and then repeat. Typically these will start with a V1 Brainstorm where the people you work with will share their ideas on how they think this problem will be solved. Sometimes you can involve users in this step and other times you might find it more valuable to do internal brainstorming first so you can cobble together a rough prototype before testing that with users.
2.) V1 Brainstorming:
V1 Brainstorming session conducted on January 5, 2021 with Jane, John Tracy and Taylor.
We came up with 8 ideas and decided to proceed with 6 of them.
Here you should link any "artifacts" you have from the brainstorming activities you did. This could be a recording of a meeting, a screenshot of a Miro/Mural board, some photographs of actual stickies on a wall. If you have more than one brainstorming session, you can decide to either consolidate it into one place or separate them via V1/V2. If you have the time, it also helps to transcribe the key findings into a more digestable format such as a table so it is easier to scan and keep track. I personally like assigning unique ID numbers to these so I can reference them in the future.

Annotating your designs is optional and can be done on a case-by-case basis. It helps people understand how these points relate but depending on the size of the project, it can get a bit hairy and difficult to maintain. The key here is to have the insights you gained from the research clearly noted down as well as the changes that those insights will inform.
4.) V2 Prototype, V3 Prototype, etc..
I won't go into details on how you do a V2 prototype documentation page as you can assume it basically just looks identical to the structure of the V1 page but the information is updated to reflect the chances in the new iteration.
After iterating through as many designs as needed for your specific project, you will get to a point where the feedback starts tapering off and it feels like you have accomplished the goals you identified in the beginning of the project! Now it is time to document your deliverables. This is the point where you are confident enough in your designs where you are ready for developers to start implementing it. For some simple projects this could be after a single iteration, but for others it could take 10+ cycles.

Designs:
Depending on your organization's design workflow and handoff process, you might need to annotate the design with additional information. This includes things like size, spacing, font styles, colours etc. Basically provide any information a developer would require to implement the design.
If you get any questions from your developer on how to implement something, document those answers here for transparency.
Q&A:
Q: What is the colour of the green button? (Jan 27, 2021 - Taylor)
A: The green should be #4CAF50
The next piece of documentation you should have is the "implementation" section. Your job as a designer does not end after it has been handed off. Once your developer tells you the implementation is done, if is your responsibility to take a look to make sure they have stayed loyal to your designs. If not, why didn't they?

Now it is up to you if you want to create a V2 to address further discrepancies after the developer has made some changes. It depends on how many outstanding changes there are.
Additionally, I have seen cases where a designer will revisit this documentation after a span of time has passed since a design has been implemented to add "reflection" notes. This could be some collected metrics that prove or dis-prove the success of the implemented designs.

Project Reflection (Jan 1, 2022)
Phew. That was long! Documentation is so much work...
I never said it would be easy. As you can see, documentation can easily spiral out of control if you are not careful, even for an example that is as simple as a small design with 5 elements and 2 goals. For this specific example, I would say this much documentation might be a bit overkill.
It is good to keep in mind that documentation is part of a workflow and is added in increments. Spending a short time documenting as you go will be much more efficient than trying to remember everything and getting it all down it at the end of a project.
One of the struggles that many have with documentation is finding the balance between added utility and it being a "waste of time". When left unchecked, good intentions could result in conflicting information that results in unnecessary maintenance and increased overhead. As I mentioned in the beginning of this post, there is no one-size-fit-all strategy. It is up to you and your teams to figure out what works for you. This could mean taking that example I shared above, omitting a few steps, diving deeper into others and perhaps adding in a few that is unique to your project.
Circling back to the example I mentioned about junior designers being stepped on. Sometimes as a junior, you can put up a fight but at some point it might be better to just relent. If that is the case (like in the example how the developer wants to go with a light green button) then that documentation is going to serve as proof that your research and work indicated that another option could work better. If a flood of complaints come in a few months after that feature is released saying the button colour makes the text hard to read, you have recourse for a low-key "I told you so".
Additionally, anyone who doesn't understand UX would have a glimpse into all the WORK you did to come up with a design! I mean, if you look at the design in question, it doesn't look like it would take more than a few hours to mock up. But if they skim through all the rationale, research and iterations you did to land on these designs they might start appreciating you just a tiny bit more.
This is not even mentioning that in the case of a blazing success... it becomes clear to all that your work was instrumental in that success! At the very least you have a lot of content to turn into a nifty case study for your portfolio.
