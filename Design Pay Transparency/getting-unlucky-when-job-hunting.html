Getting unlucky when job hunting
As I have mentioned in other posts, luck is a HUGE component of getting a job.
Referring back to this graphic, you can see that luck can be visualized as a "bonus" stat that can fill in those last few squares you need to secure your dream/first job.
Unfortunately, while the hard work is something you control, luck and privilege are usually out of your hands. Here, I just want to go over 3 of many roadblocks that you might encounter that can be chalked up to "bad luck."
1.) The Interview Anti-Loop
This theory is coined by Steve Yegge in 2008 and might apply a bit more to development jobs, but still holds some water even when applied to other professions. This can pose a potential challenge anytime your interview process consists of multiple sessions with more than one person.
I recommend reading his blog post for the full explanation, but I will provide a short TL:DR here.
The problem hinges on 2 points: 1.) You can't tell interviewers what's important unless they're specifically asking you for advice.
•
This applies to design too because of the situational nature of the work. Designers may assign different weights to different skills and this is sure to vary even among designers who work together.
2.) Every "experienced" interviewer has a set of pet subjects and possibly specific questions that he or she feels is an accurate gauge of a candidate's abilities.
•
These might be completely unrelated and even slightly opposing at times.
So what does this mean? A modified example concerning UX design might play out as follows:
Interviewer A always asks about research strategies and how you work with business stakeholders. Interviewer B always asks about detailed visual UI work and how you work with developers.
For any given candidate with both A and B on the interview loop, A and B are likely to give very different votes. A and B would probably not even hire each other, given a chance, but they both happened to go through interviewer C, who asked them both about general design process questions and iteration strategies while A and B both happened to squeak by.
According to Mr. Yegge:
That's almost always what happens when you get an offer from a tech company. You just happened to squeak by. Because of the inherently flawed nature of the interviewing process, it's highly likely that someone on the loop will be unimpressed with you, even if you are Alan Turing. Especially if you're Alan Turing, in fact, since it means you obviously don't know C++.
It basically means that seriously impressing one interviewer could cause the other to take some points off. You just had the misfortune to get caught in the Interview Anti-Loop.
If this happens, you will struggle, then be told that you were not a fit at this time, and then you will feel bad. Just as long as you don't feel meta-bad, everything is OK. You should feel good that you feel bad after this happens, because hey, it means you're human.
His advice at the end (which I echo) is do not give up and apply again when you can. This time it was bad luck, but the slate is wiped clean next time and you might get a panel of interviewers who see eye to eye.
2.) False Negatives are better than hiring a bad apple
It is incredibly expensive to hire someone who isn't right for the job. This is covered in depth in this Forbes article, but there are many hidden costs beyond that of a simple salary.
One point that I was surprised to see (but it makes a lot of sense when I think about it) is that the primary fear most employers have is — if a disengaged hire doesn't pull his weight, good employees get burned out making up for it. In the Forbes article, you see stories of poor hires subsequently causing high-performers to jump ship.
While the type of risk changes with the different job roles and required experience, the impact of a bad hire is still apparent regardless if it is an entry level, mid-level, or executive level hire.
•
Entry level hires require high investment of time and money to train.
•
Mid-level employees are expected to pull their weight with everyday work and their slack needs to be picked up by people around them.
•
A poor leader could result in a whole team under-performing.
So this is not to say you are a bad employee, but to further explain why an employer might be "afraid" to hire you if you don't happen to check off all their boxes. Theses boxes could be arbitrary, potentially based on a former employee, industry standards, or just some manager's opinions.
Sometimes you might be a great hire, but one small thing scared the employer off. While they might think there is a 99% chance you will be a good hire, they would rather pass to reduce that measly 1% of risk to 0.5%.
Unfortunately when looking at supply and demand, many employers would rather have 100 false negatives than one false positive. Until the demand changes (possible if you are very experienced in a niche field), there will always be a good chance that you were passed on because of a minuscule risk.
3.) Mr/Ms "Perfect Hire"
No one is perfect, but sometimes on paper some people just seem like a shoe-in for a role.
They might have every single "preferred" qualification, live right by the office, and also happen know someone working on the hiring team. It definitely doesn't help every other post on LinkedIn tells you to "apply even if you are not qualified!" (which is true because you might get LUCKY!) but that doesn't really account for those times you are not lucky.
Once upon a time, I thought that there are tons of people working in UX that don't have a formal education in UX. But as I spend more time in the industry, I realize that there are also plenty of people who have a very formal path into the profession. While more rare, there are many people who did masters/PhDs in related fields like Symbolic Systems, HCI, Systems Design etc. even before the 90s. In a similar vein, there are plenty of people who just have more internships under their belt or worked in an almost identical role before.
Even when I reviewed resumes for "entry-level" roles, a good 20%+ of the resumes are people with 2-4 years of various related and unrelated experience either from internship/coop or another related industry.
Maybe you were "almost perfect" for the role, fitting 10/10 requirements and 8/10 "preferred" requirements, but one person just came in with a perfect 20. Even though you ROCKED an interview, maybe they did as well. Sometimes you just get a bit unlucky. If only they didn't apply to this job, you would have probably sniped it. But the good news is that person now has a job and is out of the competition pool for now!
You gotta just keep trying.
No one is unlucky forever. The key for these things is to not give up. You can fail 100+ times, but only need one success to wipe away all those failures.
