How to balance "helping out" and looking after yourself - (especially for pro-bono work)
This topic is a bit touchy as everyone has different tolerances when it comes to work and stress. What works well for some might be very problematic for others. It is key to remember while society tends to glorify "over working," you need to figure out a balance that works for you.
Personally, I like to look at two things when considering if I want to take on a project.
1.) What is in it for me?
I don't "owe" anyone anything. If I am not being compensated for my efforts, then it is my choice to do something that should benefit me. This could be:
1.
A learning experience
2.
The work inspires me
3.
The work makes me feel good about myself for contributing to something I care about
4.
This work will open the doors to new opportunities
These are just some examples. There could be many things that motivate people to volunteer their time! The key to note here is that most of these situations should be beneficial for all the parties involved. If I can't think of a single reason a volunteer opportunity would benefit me (even if it is just "good vibes"), I would not agree to it.
There are many people out there who just might not derive any enjoyment from volunteer initiatives, such as being a mentor to people they don't know. That is 100% okay. Everyone is different and you should never feel bad about not having the extra bandwidth to go out of your way for others if it truly feels there nothing to gain for you.
2.) The importance of commitment
The exception to point 1 is if I have committed myself to something and I am capable of following through without significant harm to my sanity.
It's generally in bad form to "ghost" or "flake" on something you commit to, even if you realize later it doesn't benefit you at all. If your commitment to a task or responsibility impacts others, the least you can do is effectively communicate with them. Most people will be quite understanding if you explain that due to a re-shift of priorities, you need to give up a commitment which is causing you undue stress.
In those situations, it is generally best to try to tie off loose ends and politely excuse yourself from future responsibilities.
Your mental health should always come first. However, nothing burns bridges quite like promising to do something others are counting on and then dropping the ball with no warning, leaving everyone else scrambling.
Managing Expectations
As you gain more experience, you will start having better judgment of what commitments align with your working style. You will be able to choose ones you can stick to.
A key skill to develop which will help you for all projects going forward (but especially free ones) is managing expectations. This includes: A.) Managing your expectations for yourself - How much time and effort are you willing to put in? Where will this new workload fall in your list of priorities? etc...
B.) Managing the expectations of the people you are working with - What are you promising to deliver? What are the timelines? etc...
One of the most important things with pro-bono work is a mutual understanding of expectations and knowing things are on "your terms." This is starkly different from being paid to do work. In that scenario, you will be guaranteed an agreed-upon compensation for your work and your employer's expectations will be written in your contract.
To guarantee all parties come out of a pro-bono arrangement satisfied, you should be careful when clarifying expectations to ensure everyone is on the same page about goals, duties, and priorities.
Final note: Guilt Tripping
Generally, most people you encounter are nice.
The design industry is neat because empathy happens to be a common trait in many designers, as it is very helpful for the profession. Therefore, the community tends to be more open. However, there are always a few bad apples that actively prey on inexperienced people who might be desperate for opportunities.
If you ever reject an opportunity because you feel there is nothing in it for you or politely excuse yourself from some duties after communicating your concerns with the team, most people will be very gracious. Many will thank you for all the hard work you have done thus far, wish you the best of luck, and move on.
If you ever get angry, indignant replies, know that it is likely issues that they need to work on. As a volunteer (especially before committing to anything), you don't owe them anything other than some common courtesy.
