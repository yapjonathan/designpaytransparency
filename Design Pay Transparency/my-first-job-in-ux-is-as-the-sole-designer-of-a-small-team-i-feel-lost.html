What are some common reasons designers leave their job in UX?
To recap, I will quote that section:
It is SO COMMON to see very junior people hired on as the "sole" UX designer on a small team just because "someone needs to make the mockups."
Of course, they usually end up floundering because they have no idea what they should do and don't have any guidance. It is also hard to seek outside mentorship as those can only cover high-level things, not the day-to-day advice they need to grow when just starting out. There is no real solution to this... you either slog through tons of avoidable trial and error or have to move to a new opportunity where you can enjoy the mentorship of a senior designer.
Unfortunately, UX suffers from the "lack of junior opportunities" syndrome where companies all want senior designers, but no one is training juniors to become seniors. This just makes it very difficult for anyone trying to enter the industry.
Let's start with the bad news
There is no way to match the level of mentorship you get from working with a senior designer WITHOUT working with a senior designer.
Yes, there are tons of fantastic mentorship options out there, (Plugging ADPList here), but the main issue of these mentorship services is they only offer "high-level" mentorship.
For example if you:
•
Want guidance on different career paths
•
Want to learn more about a specific industry or employer
•
Want to have someone look at your portfolio and give you feedback
•
Want to hear about different techniques that worked well for your mentors in the past
•
Want to discuss high-level strategy like DesignOps or Design systems
Then yes, you can totally find some kick-ass mentors to help you out.
The issue I see with junior designers thrust into a role where they are calling the shots is they often don't feel confident they are calling the right shots. In school, you will learn many different design processes, but the experience of a senior designer is what helps when evaluating all the options against the unique constraints of the project, to identify what to include... and what to omit (you can see this other FAQ page linked below for details on what "experience" means in UX).
Why Experience in UX Design matters to employers
Unfortunately, these "unique constraints" are not something that can easily be explained in a 20 minute chat. Not to mention, your employer doesn't exactly want you spilling company secrets to someone who doesn't work there.
Therefore, even with 30+ years of experience, a senior designer can't make informed suggestions if they don't understand the different unique variables that factor into your project. They will only be able to offer surface-level advice, but unable to answer yes and no questions of "am I doing the right things for my project?"
What does that mean for me?
It simply means you will be doing much more trial and error, but you can still achieve the same success!
I illustrated this concept here. When you work with a senior designer, they will likely be aware of almost all the same situational variables as you. Things like:
•
Who your stakeholders are, what they want, and how to work with them
•
Timeline and resource constraints
•
Technical limitations from the engineers
•
How past design decisions will inform this current project
•
What worked in the past and what failed
And much more. These are the kind of things you can't really share to people you don't work with.
Knowing all of this and relying on their personal experiences, they will be able to come up with a plan of action for how to best structure the project. They might have ideas of different processes or exercises and be wary of certain options that just don't work because of the situational variables I mentioned above.
Additionally, senior designers or design managers typically set the flow for design work. They structure the design process which includes setting goals, timelines and expectations. These are all things that are mostly based on past experience dealing with situational variables.
So in summary, working with a senior designer smooths out your path towards a goal. It is impossible to avoid mistakes altogether because design is an ITERATIVE process. However, it is totally possible to avoid inefficiencies that come from bad goal setting, expectations, and prioritization. If you don't have someone to fill in the shoes as a mentor, you will likely just spend a lot of time working on things that don't pan out.
You will likely feel:
•
Unsure you are adding as much value as you could be. Do we NEED personas or is it just being requested because your PM thinks every design needs personas?
•
You have a never-ending workload because some things don't pan out and other things are never checked off on your to-do list.
•
Very stressed out as you deal directly with a never-ending onslaught of requests and are unsure how to prioritize things.
•
You need to "prove" the value of design work on top of doing design work. This is common with developers, product owners, or anyone else who also fancies themselves a designer with a different opinion.
Among many other stressful things.
So what... I should just quit my job?
Of course not.
This part requires some introspection because you need to be honest with yourself: are you someone who is willing to jump into the deep end and just run through a "trial by fire?"
I know plenty of designers who went down this path. Your mental health will take a hit, so that is something to be aware of. You will end up making way more mistakes which only adds to your experience down the line. You will have a trove of anecdotal battle stories to tell to young fledgling designers 10 years later. You will think fondly to a time you stayed up all night by yourself to work on something that completely flopped.
Sometimes you will be lucky enough to have very savvy co-workers. While they are not designers themselves, they are very open and maybe even proficient in design thinking methodologies. There are definitely many PMs and product owners out there that see themselves as champions for the user and an ally of UX.
There are tons of advantages of being the sole designer on a small team. In exchange for all of the stress, you get massive amounts of influence on your product and also more opportunities to fail (and learn from your failures). There is definitely no "correct" path in the UX design world, so it is all about what works for you.
Decide what is best for you
If you are someone who relishes autonomy, fancy yourself a "jack-of-all-trades," loves trying and failing (or succeeding!), and blazing your own trail, then this is a great option to cram tons of experience into a short amount of time.
If that kind of work-style stresses you out, you might seek a more "streamlined" approach where you can really focus on building specific skill sets to specialize. There, you might find a position with structured mentorship more effective.
Final note:
Once you have many years of experience under your belt, you will probably cherish being the sole designer on a small team much more as you now have the confidence that comes from experience while retaining the impact and autonomy.
I also want to re-iterate having general UX mentors is GREAT. Even if they can't tell you what you should be doing in your job, high-level advice can also help you avoid many pitfalls in your career.
