How do I "prove" the value of UX?
The good news is: as a junior designer, it usually isn't part of your job to "prove" the value of UX.
Instead, you should focus on documenting your work so you can explain why you are doing the exercises you do to gain insight, how that impacts your design decisions and how you iterate towards a solution. Basically the same things you do when you write up a portfolio case study!
Your job: Identify goals for your projects and work to achieve them
UX goals are often scoped down to things that can be tested before everything is built. You should always be starting out with some kind of "goal" and wrapping off deliverables with how you did or didn't accomplish that goal. Establishing project goals is something you do with your team. Senior UX colleagues or your manager would usually be happy to help you identify project goals when you start out.
For example, your goal for a project could be:
A technically feasible design where X-type users can successfully perform actions ABC.
Your follow up statement with the final deliverables could be
After testing with 6 X-type users, we confirmed that with this design, 100% of them were able to successfully perform actions ABC. The designs are approved by the engineering team and deemed technically feasible.
It can take over a year for something to get developed and shipped. Once your design is out in the wild, there could be more goals that get spun off into projects. The gist of it is the same. You work with your PM and team to identify goals and then work to achieve them, usually through UX processes.
The better your work process documentation is as a junior, the more appreciative your managers will be as it makes their job easier. They will be the ones who need to "prove" the value of UX to everyone else in the org. The better your work, the more ammunition they will have to show the "non-believers" these iterations you work on is driving a better product design!
Let's run through a very simplified example:
Imagine you are unsure about where to put a "close" button in a modal.
You are debating about the top left corner, or top right corner. As part of the testing process, you make a prototype with both options and test it with some users. It turns out that with your specific users, it made much more sense for it to be the top right because they primarily use Windows OS which tends to have a close button on the top right.
During a presentation, a stakeholder looks up from his Macbook and says "it makes much more sense to have it on the top left corner because that is where it always is." Instead of it devolving into a "you vs. stakeholder" opinion-off, you can just pull out the documentation from the research session you conducted and show them 10/10 users expected the button to be on the top right.
Bam, user research nipping an argument in the bud. Everyone in the room is now a bit more aware of the thought process that goes into these decisions and respects the work you do just a tiny bit more.
The best UX is proactive, not reactive
It is harder to sell the concept of preventing problems from becoming problems. Similar to other proactive things like "environmental sustainability" and "pandemic preparation," it is hard to put a precise value of how much money/lives we could have saved if we done things differently or how much more it would have cost if we did things worse.
The best tool for proving the "value" of UX is through good documentation of our work so we can present compelling case studies to bolster the case for future work.
