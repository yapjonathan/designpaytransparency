Why do I feel like my designs are just not as "good" as others?
Similar to the feeling of impostor syndrome I cover in Imposter Syndrome as a UX Designer, this post looks at a specific feeling that all designers have from time to time.
Regardless how hard I push myself, I feel like I keep hitting a wall and can’t get my work to the level where I would be proud of it.
And trust me, I have been there. Multiple times.
The issue with many open-ended projects is that there are so many different options to consider. A single person is only capable of holding so much in their head. Those opinions are highly informed by your unique upbringing and situation.
As a single brain, you can only push an idea forward so much until they hit that dreaded wall. This is the point where you might have exhausted all the "inspiration" you have access to at any point in time. You look at your design and can't really think of ways it could be improved.
This is where that term "a second pair of eyes" comes in clutch. Bringing in a diverse selection of other people can help you spot possibilities and opportunities you never even thought of. Even better, these insights often lead you to come up with more ideas. They bounce back and forth until you suddenly have a trove of options you can't wait to try.
Design is a team sport
Regardless of how overused this statement is, it remains true. It is rare (and unhealthy!) for designers to be slaving away by themselves in a silo, expected to produce great results the first time they try. The reason so many designs are amazing is because of the iterative process that pushes it forward. It is too easy to fall into the mindset of "I" am not good enough... but you shouldn't bear this burden by yourself.
The V1 of something is never good, but you keep trying, get feedback, make improvements... and eventually you get there. Feedback doesn't need to come from other designers either. The people who interact with your design, implement it, sell it, etc. all provide valuable feedback that really takes the design to the next level.
The idea of being "better at design" is dangerous
Yes, there are many fundamental technical skills that are important to designers. A deep understanding of theories, principles, and methodologies such as user-centered design thinking, information hierarchy, colour theory, etc. are all knowledge-based things you can always learn more about. Putting them into action increases your capabilities as a designer.
What these things are not, though, is categorizing "producing better designs" as a skill itself. If anything, it comes down to competence in the processes that result in better designs. Just because a designer has worked at name-brand companies and has 20 years of experience does not mean they can lock themselves in a closet and emerge with the "best design ever" in a few days.
One of the defining traits of a good designer is the ability to work well with others. The ability to get the most out of the people they work with is something that will always be important in a field where actions are informed by intent. Good designers inspire others to come up with good feedback and ideas. They are able to filter and incorporate it, continually driving a design closer to a goal.
How to get that feedback
I have witnessed with my own eyes people who are usually quiet, suddenly opening up into a torrent of ideas and opinions under the right conditions. Sure, there are a few cases out there where someone really prefers to keep their ideas close, but often you will find that under the right circumstances, everyone has an opinion to share.
Here are some things you could think about when asking for feedback (in no particular order):
1.
People need to feel SAFE giving feedback
•
This means you should never respond negatively to someone's feedback, even if you disagree. Lashing out to someone (even small tics like "rolling your eyes") who is providing candid feedback is a surefire way to make them scared to speak their mind again.
•
Never "retaliate" to their feedback. It is fine to probe deeper to uncover details on why they think a certain way, but do not argue with them as this is a matter of their personal opinion.
2.) People want to feel their feedback is VALUABLE
•
Never "dismiss" someone's feedback outright. Even if you disagree, it should be noted down and they should be thanked for providing it. If anything, the best time to drill deeper is why they disagree to gently figure out why.
•
It is always nice to write down their feedback and give them access to a copy of it (without any of your personal notes of course). People will appreciate you took the time to write it all out and it acts as a good chance for them to point out any errors in communication or understanding.
3.) People sometimes need a little confidence boost
•
Sometimes an icebreaker or some small talk can do wonders in easing someone's nerves. If they are not used to being asked for feedback, they might find the experience intimidating. Make sure to reassure them they are in friendly company.
•
In the scenario where you notice some people "overpowering" others, some facilitation skills may come in handy to ensure everyone has a chance to voice their opinion.
4.) People want to understand WHY they are being asked for feedback
•
This includes going over ground rules, establishing expectations, and making sure everyone understands the problem and goals.
•
If there is a specific way you want people to voice their feedback (such as following a framework), then you need to explain it in a way they understand and feel comfortable with.
These are just a few of many tips that could help you get that juicy feedback to elevate your designs to the next level!
Putting it all together
After that long segue into tips for collecting feedback, let's circle back to why feedback is important for keeping your sanity as a designer.
You are NOT alone and NOT expected to come up with all your designs relying solely by yourself. When you look at dribbble images, often they are the results of years of experience, multiple iterations, AND the feedback of many.
When you feel stuck, reach out! A great place to start is in a design community like Design Buddies.
