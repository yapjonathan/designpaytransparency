How do I find freelance work in UX?
A horrible piece of advice I see given to many people starting out in UX is to go look for freelance work (this is different if you already have freelance work opportunities, that is a different situation alltogether). I'd say 9/10 times these fresh designers with no prior experience end up rushing on to marketplaces like fiverr and upwork to start bidding on a handful of freelance contracts.
Similar to the topic My first job in UX is as the sole designer of a small team... I feel lost, it is incredibly hard to start out freelancing in this industry with almost no guidance. While it is totally possible to make a career out of freelancing contracts in UX, it is very hard to START for many reasons.
Unfortunately, there is no secret to finding well-paying clients with interesting work. At least for for those new to the industry. There is however, some general criteria that helps a freelance UX designer stand out, so let's start with that.
A glimpse into the mind of a potential client
Let's start by putting ourselves in a potential client's point of view. For the purpose of this post, I have whipped up a simple persona. Meet Jen.
Sure, Jen is only one of the many different types of potential clients on these freelancing sites. She is here because you need to ask yourself. Why would someone look to hire a freelancer on a freelancing website instead of an actual employee? Most likely it would be because they:
1.) Don't have the financial or time resources to hire a full-time employee
2.) Have a one-off project where they only need someone for a short period of time
3.) Don't know anyone in their current network that they trust to do the work to their standards and they don't have the skills and/or time to do it themselves (if they could they would do it themselves).
So going back to Jen, you will see she fits into these boxes.
To satisfy their goals and address their fears, they would likely have a prioritized laundry list of criteria they will judge prospective candidates on. I imagine it might look something like:
1.) Competitive Price - For the strict budget
2.) Good testimonials - To reduce fear of a bad and/or slow hire
3.) Seems professional with good communication skills - To ensure they understand the vision
4.) Visually impressive portfolio of past projects - To make sure the end result is beautiful
So as a new designer how do you compete on these 4 criteria?
1.) Price
In general you never want to be in a position where you are competing on price alone. Unless you live in an incredibly low cost-of-living area, you will likely not be able to win purely with cheap labour. For example here are some "templated" design services offered on upwork. For under $100, someone looking for a simple mockup could be connected with a "modern web design".
Additionally, you will have to compete with experienced designers like this one (out of Ukraine) that only bills at $25 an hour and has a 100% success rate after 122 jobs!
A quick look at their profile showed a plethora of beautiful UI displays and 5-star reviews.
Another thing to consider is even if you set your prices at rock bottom, a client runs the risk of a bad designer wasting their time. Why would they run the risk of hiring someone with no past jobs? I can almost guarantee you that a client whose number 1 priority is cost will not be a fun client to work with.
2.) Good Testimonials
When you start out, you will obviously not have any of these. You need to do projects to slowly build this up. This is yet another reason why it is generally a great idea to start your career freelancing as you could also be building this trust up in a more streamlined and organic way by working a regular full-time job and interacting and establishing a presence within design communities.
If you take a look at some of the designers charging a higher rate, you will see that they tend to have either plenty of jobs or 0 jobs.
While it is not impossible, it is very difficult to jump into professional work with no prior experience and succeed on your first try. You will likely need to slog through many unfulfilling projects to build up your reputation before you appear near the top of the list of "recommended" designers in these freelancing platforms.
3.) Communication skills
I won't touch too much on this one. Effective communication is crucial to just about every job out there. This is something that you work on both in your personal life, but also can work on in your "professional" life. There are tips and tricks that are easy to pick up organically working in an office environment, but you can easily substitute working with other professionals in a team environment. Some important things I can list off the top of my head include:
•
How to actively listen and take notes when someone else is explaining something
•
How to clarify and get on the same page with someone after listening to them
•
How to structure conversation to effectively communicate your points
•
How to prioritize and align expectations with multiple parties
•
How to reassure others that work is on track as per expectations
These are all things that would help you succeed as a freelancer, but it is up to the individual to build competence in these things if they have not worked as a professional in the UX industries yet.
4.) Visually Impressive Portfolio
This is probably the easiest hurdle to get past as a new designer, as it is EASY to just whip up things that look nice. Just watch this "How to be popular on Dribble" video and pop out some nice mockups.
But the TRUE problem of competing with visuals as a new designer is.. you are shooting yourself in the foot if you ever want to transition into a full-time UX role in the future.
One of the BIGGEST problems with freelance UX design contracts is that more often than not, they are actually looking for UI design. They probably don't care about your "design process" as long as the results fit in their narrow vision of how they picture their product.
Just because the deliverables an an interactive Figma prototype does NOT mean that they are looking for a well-rounded UX designer to methodically work through an iterative research-backed process to dig up the best possible experiences for users.
Because the client is not looking for long-term talent that will drive the design of a product from incubation to post-delivery, they will also not care as much about the "big picture" thinking that is at the heart of UX. Instead they will likely be seeking a "mockup machine" candidate that can just take their vision and turn it into something more beautiful.
You will probably not get to talk to users and it is unlikely you will get to test anything. The lack of user-centered design process in most freelance projects will definitely be reflected in your portfolio if you don't have any other solid projects to compensate.
Okay, so I know I painted "freelancing" in a very unfavourable light in this post but I also have lots of caveats to these thoughts.
1.) Freelancing can be great for experienced designers
Once you have a few years of experience under your belt as a full-time designer elsewhere, you will likely have the experience and skills to feel confident about tackling freelance contracts. Through your reputation working in the industry, you will also likely have access to better opportunities through your personal and professional network.
Many people prefer freelancing because of the flexibility. It is amazing to be able to pick up projects on an as-needed basis and then take long periods of time off to pursue other things. Additionally, it could mean getting to work on a wide variety of projects and explore different industries instead of laser-focusing on one.
Larger well-known companies are known to hire freelancers in a few cases:
•
To backfill short term leaves (such as a Maternity leave). This offers the best of both worlds as you get the full experience of being embedded in a large team and a long-term project, but are limited to a short 6-9 month contract.
•
To fill a very niche role as a consultant. An example of this could be a contract to conduct a heuristic analysis + insights and recommendations for a product.
•
To temporarily support an initiative that has not received full funding yet. This could be to push out a MVP or POC of an idea. If the idea fails to gain traction, there is no complication in laying off full-time staff.
However, for these scenarios they ONLY hire experienced designers (5+ years) in these cases as they want someone with a proven track record who is capable of asking the right questions, getting up to speed and jumping right in with little to no guidance.
2.) Not everyone has the luxury of choice
Even though in my opinion freelancing is not an ideal path for a fresh UX designer, I recognize not everyone has the luxury to turn their nose at an opportunity to make a living.
For example, I know there are many geographical markets out there that simply don't have UX jobs. Small towns and cities that do not have white collar service jobs and a tech scene. In those cases, if someone can't find a remote opportunity, freelancing might be the only viable path.
Additionally, someone in a tight financial situation who can't secure full-time opportunities still needs to pay rent. Freelancing is a flexible and potentially a lucrative source of revenue with enough time and commitment.
3.) Freelancing works better for other types of work (such as Graphic Design)
The nature of UX being a continuous process makes it difficult to produce good results in a one-off project. In contrast, there are many other types of work out there that work much better on a freelance model. Some that I can think of include graphic design, voiceovers, and copywriting.
Basically anything that can be a "one and done" project. For example, a project to design a logo would end once the logo is finalized. There is not a need for continuous follow-up work afterwards. The content in this FAQ piece is referring specifically to User Experience work.
4.) You can totally succeed with enough hard work
Like in that post for working as the sole designer in a startup, just because a path is difficult does not mean it is a bad path. Being aware of many of the points I highlight here is a good first step. The next step is to problem-solve your way around them.
There is a lot to gain from taking the path less travelled, and with enough drive, determination and hard work, all of the obstacles I mention here can be overcome. I just want to point out this path might not be for everyone.
In conclusion
I have not answered the question to this post because there is no easy answer.
There is no silver bullet to find well-paying clients with interesting projects. The closest thing to that is personal connections.
Instead, I hope you enjoy this long and winding post about things to consider when trying to start your career with freelance work.
