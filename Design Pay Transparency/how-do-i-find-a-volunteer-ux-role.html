How do I find a volunteer UX role?
Too many people starting out in UX are looking for "structure" in the sense "once I get accepted as a volunteer, they will take my hand, guide me through the correct steps, and tell me what I need to do to succeed."
These highly structured volunteer opportunities definitely exist, likely for well-established organizations who have paid people in charge of volunteers. But most likely you will find those organizations are looking more for "grunt" volunteers vs. "innovation" volunteers. They are sourcing volunteers to do the work no one else wants to do. For example, the role might just involve you making social media post designs every other day.
There is absolutely NOTHING WRONG with experience as a "grunt" volunteer. Bonus points if it is for a cause you feel strongly about and if the organization has some clout. At the very least, you will get to work with others, which is something employers love to see. This is mentioned in the FAQ topic below:
Why Experience in UX Design matters to employers
Unfortunately, most volunteer orgs (outside of the well-established ones with proper internship programs) don't completely have it all figured out. They probably don't fully understand they need. They might have an idea of the "results" they want, but the real opportunity is you pitching your ideas and getting them to support you.
In my opinion, it might be easier to sit down and think about what kind of project YOU want to work on.
Next, brainstorm a list of nonprofits, small businesses, orgs etc. you can pitch it to. At the end of the day, UX needs to drive value to users. If you are offering your services for free, many groups/organizations would happily accept your help if you can really identify their problems and present a compelling and realistic solution to look into.
I personally think the best volunteer opportunities are through your personal network.
It can be for a friend's small business (if you know the owners of a local cafe, etc.) or if anyone you know is trying to found their own startup. Maybe someone you know physically volunteers somewhere you have good vibes for, (like a local humane society).
I saw a good example in a portfolio where a girl approached her local humane society with a "Merch" idea to sell some calendars to raise funds. It is pretty common for human societies to sell calendars, but her local branch never had anyone to take the lead on that initiative.
She worked through some design exercises to figure out what has worked well in the past and what incentivized their donors. They sent out some short surveys and conducted focus groups on what locals would love to see in a calendar. After some design iterations, this culminated with her designing and producing a calendar as well as an online presence including social media content.
The project ended with tangible impact. They had metrics that showed over the course of X years, they were able to raise X money from this specific project that resulted in X dogs finding new homes.
I remember being super impressed, as this portfolio piece from a junior designer shows initiative, creative thinking, process, teamwork AND impact. The project was also well-documented in the portfolio in a clean, visual manner. It shows way better than just a bootcamp hypothetical project or a "redesign" because they identified a real goal, users, and actually shipped a product that made an impact.
You just have to go for it!
There is no downside in approaching small businesses to see if you could help them with anything! Worst case, they say no.
A big opportunity these days is helping really old fashioned businesses with an online presence. For example: Getting them on to food delivery platforms etc.
Here is a hypothetical to inspire you:
Many ethnic restaurants in smaller areas might not have an online menu. If they had a nice website where there are beautiful pictures of their food and an easy way to submit orders, maybe they will get more business.
In this specific example, the UX behind it is people are visual when it comes to ordering food. What if you were to find/conduct some research showing that having images or videos of food will produce a superior reaction in people vs. just listing the options out in text. The value of different menu layouts featuring more images could result in more people wanting to eat that food, resulting in more sales, etc. This, of course, is a hypothetical idea, but could also be a cool opportunity to run usability tests on something as simple as a restaurant menu!
You don't need much to identify a cool potential UX project.
You want to
1.
Find a need
2.
Do some research that proves there is a need
3.
Identify who the users are and who you specifically want to target
4.
Brainstorm how you can fulfill that need for the different users
5.
Validate these ideas by testing them with users (could be prototypes, storyboards, mockups etc.)
6.
Iterate on your solutions until you find something that works well for the needs/users you established
7.
Try to get it implemented and follow up on results. Bonus points if you can get feedback and do another iteration!
Of course, this is just a summary. There are plenty of resources out there for how to structure a UX process and it varies for every project. The point is "the best type of project is one where you can actually perform these iteration cycles with the users you identify."
The problem with a lot of "redesigns" is they are based off of either only the designer's own opinions or they don't show enough iteration. They jump straight from "here is the problem" to "I have identified THE solution." But in the real world, often most of the heavy lifting is the stuff in between.
Hopefully this gives you a bit of inspiration into how you can identify cool UX volunteer opportunities that both drive impact AND look great in a portfolio
